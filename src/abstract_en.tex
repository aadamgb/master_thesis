Model-free \ac{RL} has advanced drone racing performance but remains sample inefficient.
Geometric control offers the smoothness and aggressiveness that racing demands, but it is limited by manual gain tuning. 
This thesis investigates whether direct \ac{BPTT} can improve sample efficiency over standard \ac{PPO} while matching its performance, and whether learned adaptive gains can outperform fixed ones in geometric control. 
We build a differentiable quadrotor simulator in PyTorch and compare two training tasks and four control strategies. 
We find that \ac{BPTT} is up to $\mathbf{5\times}$ more sample efficient in a single-gate scenario, but it behaves greedily and fails to outperform \ac{PPO} on full racing tracks.
Adaptive gains likewise do not outperform fixed ones with our formulation. 
However, a fixed gain controller optimized with \ac{PPO} still matches state-of-the-art results, establishing our simulator as a foundation for further research.
