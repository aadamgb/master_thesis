\chapter{Methodology\label{chap:how_to}}

\section{Problem definition}
We define a domain as a specific subset of possible \ac{POMDP} environments. 
While a complete \ac{POMDP} is formally characterized by an 8-tuple~\cite{}, 
we focus on a reduced 6-tuple representation that captures the essential elements 
for our domain definition. A standard domain is characterized by 
$\mathcal{D} = (\mathcal{S}, \mathcal{A}, \mathcal{O}, P, O, R)$ where:

\begin{itemize}
    \item $\mathbf{\mathcal{S}:}$ denotes a set of states called the state space.
    \item $\mathbf{\mathcal{A}:}$ is the set of actions called the action space.
    \item $\mathbf{\mathcal{O}:}$ is the set of observations called the observation space.
    \item $\mathbf{P(s'|s, a):}$ is the probability that a given action $a \in A$, at state $s \in S$ will lead to a new state $s' \subset S$. 
    \item $\mathbf{O(o|s', a):}$ is the observation probability, representing the probability of observing $o \in \mathcal{O}$ after taking action $a$ and transitioning to state $s'$.
    \item $\mathbf{R(s',s,a):}$ is the immediate reward, or expected inmediate reward, received for transitioning from $s$ to $s'$ after performing action $a$. 
\end{itemize}
From the source domain, $\mathcal{D}_s$, (i.e. simulation), we can control a set of N parameters of the variable aspects of each environment that define a configuration vector, $\boldsymbol{\xi} = [\xi_i, \xi_{i+1}, ..., \xi_N]$.
Examples of $\xi_i$ can include:

\begin{tabular}{c|c}

    Physical properties & $\xi_{\text{mass}}, \ \xi_{\text{inertia}}, \ \xi_{\text{drag}}, \ \xi_{\text{gravity}}$ \\
    Perception properties & $\xi_{\text{sensor\_noise}}, \ \xi_{\text{sensor\_latency}}$ \\
    Viual properties & $\xi_{\text{lighting}}, \ \xi_{\text{texture}}, \ \xi_{\text{fov}}$ \\
    Scene properties & $\xi_{\text{gate\_pos}}, \ \xi_{\text{gate\_size}}, , \ \xi_{\text{gate\_num}}$ \\
    
\end{tabular}

Each configuration is sampled from a randomization space, $\boldsymbol{\xi} \in \Xi \subset \mathbb{R}^N$, with a probability distribution $\boldsymbol{\xi} \sim p(\boldsymbol{\xi})$.
Where the parameters are bounded to be within an interval $\xi_i \in [\xi_i^{\text{low}}, \xi_i^{\text{high}}]$. 

At the start of every training episode, a new environment insance is created by sampling a new configuration vector $\boldsymbol{\xi}$ from the distribution $p(\boldsymbol{\xi})$. 
Therefore, the agent will interact with a specific \ac{POMDP} environment, that is to say, a specific domain $\mathcal{D}_{\boldsymbol{\xi}}$. 
This way, the agent can collect data from a vareity of environments that otherwise would reamain unexplored.

Hereby, during training, the neural network weights and biases $\theta$ are optimized to maximize the expected reward $R$ \textbf{across a distribution of randomized domains} \cite{weng2019DR}.
With the aim of finding the optimal, $\theta^*$ that will lead to the optimal parametrized policy $\pi_{\theta}^*$.
\begin{equation}\label{opt_theta}
    \theta ^* = \arg \max_{\theta} \mathbb{E}_{\boldsymbol{\xi}\sim\Xi} \left[\mathbb{E}_{\pi_\theta,\tau\sim D_\xi}[R(\tau)] \right]
\end{equation}
Where $R(\tau) = \sum_{t=0}^{n} \gamma^t r_{t}$ is the cummulative discounted reward over a trajectory (or sequence) of states and actions $\tau = (s_0,a_0,s_1,a_1,...)$, for the episode length $n$.
% $R(\tau)  = \sum_{t=0}^{n} \gamma^t r_{t}$

% \[ R(\tau)  = \sum_{t=0}^{n} \gamma^t r_{t}\]
As illustrated in \autoref{domains} and described in \autoref{opt_theta}, the policy is exposed to a variety of domains to promote generalization and improve robustness, thereby increasing the likelihood of successful transfer to the real environment. 
However, this strategy further reduces the sample efficiency of \ac{RL} and increases training time. 
Therefore, it is essential to properly bound the randomization space $\Xi$ so that it encompasses only the minimum necessary range required to include the real domain.

\section{Quadcopter model}\label{QuadModel}
In the simulated environments, the quadcopter is modeeld as a 6 \ac{dof}, rigid body. 
The state of the quadcopter is defined by its position $\mathbf{p}$, velocity $\mathbf{v}$, orientation $\mathbf{q}$, body rates $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ and rotor speeds $\mathbf{\Omega}$.
Namely, $\mathbf{x} = [\mathbf{p}, \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{q}, \boldsymbol{\omega}, \mathbf{\Omega}]^T$. 
The position, velocity and acceleration are respresented in the world frame $\mathcal{W} \in \mathbb{R}^3$, while the body rates in the body frame $\mathcal{B} \in \mathbb{R}^3$.
The orientation is described by an unit quaternion rotation $\mathbf{q} \in \mathbb{SO}(3)$. 
Hence, the kinematics and dynamics of the drone are:
\hspace{-5cm}
\begin{minipage}{0.5\textwidth}
\begin{equation}
  \dot{\mathbf{p}}^{\mathcal{W}} = \mathbf{v}^{\mathcal{W}}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
  \dot{\mathbf{q}} = \tfrac{1}{2}\ \mathbf{q} \odot \begin{bmatrix} 0 & \boldsymbol{\omega}^{\mathcal{B}}\end{bmatrix}^T
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
  \dot{\mathbf{v}}^{\mathcal{W}} = \frac{R(\mathbf{q})}{m}\left(\mathbf{f}_T^{\mathcal{B}} + \mathbf{f}_D^{\mathcal{B}}\right) + \mathbf{g}^{\mathcal{W}}
\end{equation}
\end{minipage}%
\begin{minipage}{0.5\textwidth}
\begin{equation}
  \dot{\boldsymbol{\omega}}^{\mathcal{B}} = J^{-1}\left(\boldsymbol{\tau}^{\mathcal{B}} - \boldsymbol{\omega}^{\mathcal{B}} \times J\boldsymbol{\omega}^{\mathcal{B}}\right)
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
  \dot{\mathbf{\Omega}} = \frac{1}{k_{\mathrm{m}}} \left(\mathbf{\Omega}_c - \mathbf{\Omega}\right)
\end{equation}
\end{minipage}
Where, $\odot$ represents quaternion multiplication, 
$R(\mathbf{q})$ is the rotational matrix from body frame $\mathcal{B}$ to world frame $\mathcal{W}$ at orientation $\mathbf{q}$,
$J$ is the diagonal inertia matrix,
$\mathbf{f}_T^\mathcal{B}$ and $\mathbf{f}_D^\mathcal{B}$ are the collective thrust and drag respectively, 
$k_m$ the motor time constant,
$m$ the qudcopter's mass and $\mathbf{g}$ is gravity.

In the body frame $\mathcal{B}$, the individual motor thrusts $T_i, \ i=1,..,4$, are assumed to be perpendicular to the $\mathbf{xy}$ plane.
The collective thrust $\mathbf{f}_T^\mathcal{B}$, and torques $\boldsymbol{\tau}^\mathcal{B}$, can be maped from the individual motor thrusts with the allocation matrix $\Gamma$.

\begin{equation}
  \begin{bmatrix}
    \mathbf{f}^\mathcal{B}_T \\
    \tau_x \\
    \tau_y \\
    \tau_z \\
  \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}
    1 & 1 & 1 & 1 \\
    -d/\sqrt{2} & d/\sqrt{2} &d/\sqrt{2} & -d/\sqrt{2} \\
    -d/\sqrt{2} & d/\sqrt{2} &-d/\sqrt{2} & d/\sqrt{2} \\
    c_{tf} & c_{tf} & c_{tf} & c_{tf} \\
  \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix}
    T_1 \\ T_2 \\ T_3 \\ T_4
  \end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}
Being $d$ the arm length and $c_{tf}$ the linear torque constant.
Likewise, the indidual motror thrusts $T_i$, can be mapped from the rotor speeds $\Omega_i$ with the thrust map coefficents, $\mathbf{k_T} = [a_0,\ a_1,\ a_2]$ that are determined experimentally fitting a quadratic curve.

\begin{equation}
  T_i = \mathbf{k_T} \begin{bmatrix}
    \Omega_i^2 \\ \Omega_i \\ 1
  \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}
  a_2 & a_1 & a_0
  \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix}
    \Omega_i^2 \\ \Omega_i \\ 1
  \end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}
The total drag force, $\mathbf{f}_D^\mathcal{B}$ is calculated using a simplified aerodynamic model function of the dron's linear velocity and proportional to the drag coefficents.

\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{f}_{D}^\mathcal{B} = \begin{bmatrix}
      C_{D_x} & 0 & 0 \\
      0 & C_{D_y} & 0 \\
      0 & 0 & C_{D_z} \\
    \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix}
      v_x^\mathcal{B} \\ v_x^\mathcal{B} \\ v_x^\mathcal{B}
    \end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}

\section{\ac{RL} Set-Up}

Although the simplified quadcopter model defined in \ref{QuadModel} is used to represent the drone dynamics in simulation, the \ac{RL} setup employed in this project is \textbf{model-free}.
Specifically, it consists of an \ac{AC} structure optimized using \ac{PPO}~\cite{}.
The implementation relies on Stable-Baselines3~\cite{} to interface with a custom C++ Gymnasium environment (based on flightmare~\cite{}) and to train the \ac{AC} networks using \ac{PPO}.

The actor policy netwrok  $\pi_\theta$, is defined by a \ac{MLP} that takes an observation vector at time step $t$, \autoref{obs_vec} of 31 inputs. The observation vector is sampled from a bounded observation space $o_t\in \mathcal{O} \subset \mathbb{R}^{31}$.


\begin{equation}\label{obs_vec}
  o_t = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{p}_t & \mathbf{v}_t & \text{vec}R(\mathbf{q})_t & u_{t-1} & 
    \mathbf{p_{g}}^{TL}_t & \mathbf{p_{g}}^{TR}_t & \mathbf{p_{g}}^{BL}_t & \mathbf{p_{g}}^{BL}_t
    & \mathbf{p}_t^{\text{look}}
  \end{bmatrix}^T
\end{equation}
Where $u_{t-1}$ is the throttle motor command at the prevous time step. 
$\mathbf{p_{g}}^X_t$ are  relative positions of the corners of the target gate at time $t$ with respect the body frame $\mathcal{B}$.
And, $\mathbf{p}_t^{\text{look}}$ is the look at position of the target gate at time $t$, also in the body frame.
The input layer is followed by three hidden layers of 128 neurons each, and it outputs 4 neurons. 
The outputs are the \ac{CTBR}, sampled from a bounded action space $a_t \in \mathcal{A} \subset \mathbb{R}^4$.
\begin{equation}\label{act_vec}
  a_t = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{f}_{Tt} & \boldsymbol{\omega}_t 
  \end{bmatrix}^T
\end{equation}
