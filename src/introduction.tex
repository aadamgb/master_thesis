\section{Background}

\lettrine[lines=2,lhang=0.2]{\textbf{\Huge A}}{DR} has emerged as a compelling platform in robotics to develop and deploy novel control techniques in real-world scenarios.
The aim of this sport is to navigate through a sequence of gates in minimum time (see \autoref{a2rl}), pushing the platform’s capabilities to their limits~\cite{hanover_autonomous_2024}.
To maintain the platform light and agile, the hardware is kept minimal; a typical \ac{ADR} drone consists of a quadrotor equipped with an RGB camera, an \ac{IMU}, an onboard computer, a flight controller, brushless \ac{DC} motors with propellers, and an \ac{ESC} all powered by a \ac{LiPo} battery~\cite{hert_mrs_2023,foehn_agilicious_2022}.

\ac{ADR} presents several challenges, which can be divided into the following subgroups depicted in \autoref{fig:intro}.

    \label{fig:intro}

The control stack begins at the \textbf{perception} level.
This stage involves processing sensory data from the camera and IMU, which is inherently noisy and uncertain.
The main challenge at this level is to filter and fuse this data to estimate both the position of the gates and the state of the drone.
The fusion of camera and IMU measurements is known as \ac{VIO}.
State estimation is typically performed using filtering techniques that combine a system model with sensor measurements, such as a Kalman Filter~\cite{kalman} or variants~\cite{wan_unscented_2000}.
Given an estimate of the drone state and the environment, the next step is \textbf{planning}.
The objective in \ac{ADR} is to compute a minimum-time trajectory that passes through the gates while avoiding collisions.
Trajectory planning can be computationally demanding, making efficient approaches particularly important.
The final stage of the pipeline is \textbf{control}.
With the planned trajectory, the goal is to obtain the control inputs to track it with minimal error.
This task is challenging due to the highly nonlinear dynamics of the quadrotor.
Additionally, the system is underactuated, with only four out of the six \ac{dof} directly controllable.
Moreover, yaw control authority is typically limited, and actuator saturation further constrains performance.

These problems are common in robotics, and a variety of methods have been proposed to address them.
Learning-based methods, particularly those relying on using a \ac{NN}, are gaining popularity due to their low-latency inference and robustness to noise.
Individual components of the navigation stack can be learned by the \ac{NN}s.
For instance, \ac{CNN}s can process \ac{VIO} data to detect gates, while \ac{MLP}s  are frequently used for planning and/or control.
Combinations of multiple stages, such as perception and planning or planning and control, can also be learned jointly.
In some cases, the entire pipeline is learned end-to-end \cite{hanover_autonomous_2024}.
Learning-based approaches offer several advantages; however, training these models typically requires large amounts of data, and current optimization methods can be sample inefficient.
Since collecting such data in real-world settings is often impractical, simulated environments that approximate real-world distributions are used, enabling thousands of parallel training instances.
Recent research hasexplored the use of \ac{ADE} supported by \ac{ML} frameworks such as \cite{paszke_pytorch_2019,jax2018github,abadi_tensorflow_2016} to propagate gradients from a cost function to \ac{NN} parameters.
This approach significantly improves sample efficiency~\cite{zhang_back_2025} and avoids treating the quadrotor dynamics as a black box.
In this thesis, we investigate this direction. We focus on the use of differentiability to learn the control component.
We aim to demonstrate that this approach is a promising avenue for research in robotics.

\section{Related Work}

The evolution of \ac{ADR} has progressed from early academic benchmarks in the IROS competitions between 2016 and 2018 \cite{iros2016, iros2017}, where drones reached speeds of only a few meters per second, to highly professionalized racing, where autonomous drones can exceed speeds of $150\ \mathrm{km/h}$ and outperform professional human pilots (see \autoref{fig:introo}).

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \resizebox{1.0\textwidth}{!}{\input{fig/tikz/timeline.tex}}
    \caption{Maximum achieved speeds in drone racing competitions over time (adapted from~\cite{hanover_autonomous_2024}). }
    \label{fig:introo}
\end{figure}

Since the AlphaPilot competition in 2019~\cite{lockheedmartinAlphaPilotDrone}, there has been a clear trend toward incorporating learning-based components into the navigation stack.
In \cite{foehnalphapilot2021}, the pipeline combines \ac{VIO} and gate detections within an \ac{EKF} for state estimation, followed by trajectory planning using sampling-based receding horizon methods.
Control is implemented through a cascade of position and attitude controllers, while \ac{NN}s are used exclusively for visual gate detection.
Subsequent work in \cite{kaufmannchampion-level2023} retains a similar perception pipeline but replaces planning and control with a feedforward \ac{MLP}, optimized using \ac{PPO} \cite{ppo} and achieve champion-level performance.

In a related setup, \cite{songreaching2023} demonstrate that model-free \ac{RL} can outperform traditional optimal control approaches.
The authors attribute the increase in performance of \ac{RL} to two main factors.
First, the ability of model-free \ac{RL} to directly formulate the task goal as part of the optimization function without decomposing the problem into planning and control.
This allows their policy to explore a broader range of behaviours that provide robustness to unmodeled dynamic effects.
Furthermore, since \ac{RL} is usually modeled as a Markov Decision Process, the transition model is probabilistic, which enables \ac{DR}.
"By training in randomized environments, the agent becomes more robust and adaptable, making it more effective at handling disturbances and model uncertainty"~\cite{songreaching2023}.

On a similar note, recent approaches have explored model-based \ac{RL}, where a learned world model enables training through imagined rollouts.
In \cite{romero_dream_2025, verraest_skydreamer_2025}, the authors adopt an actor-critic architecture based on Dreamer~\cite{hafner_mastering_2024}.
This approach also achieves champion-level performance and has the advantage of directly mapping visual data (raw pixel information) to motor commands, skipping the need for an intermediate controller.
While these methods achieve strong performance, they suffer from long training times and limited sample efficiency; for instance, training in \cite{verraest_skydreamer_2025} can take up to 50 hours, significantly slowing the research cycle.

To address these limitations, recent work has shifted toward differentiable approaches that enable the backpropagation of gradients from an objective function through the entire navigation stack; this is known as \ac{BPTT}.
This paradigm avoids treating the quadrotor dynamics as a black-box and showed improvements in sample efficiency by leveraging first-order gradients instead of relying on zeroth-order optimization.
The work of \cite{zhang_back_2025} pioneered this research direction, achieving agile vision-based flight in cluttered environments using a \ac{CRNN} and a simplified double-integrator model.
Their policy achieves zero-shot generalization to the real world; that is, no fine-tuning or retraining is required for policy deployment.
In their setup, they run the policy on a budget-friendly microprocessor and a depth camera, allowing for robust obstacle avoidance without needing an \ac{IMU} or GPS.
Additionally, they deploy the policy simultaneously on different drones and observe emerging behaviours such as waiting for other agents to cross a gate without explicitly adding collaborative terms to the loss function.

Another differentiable simulation framework is introduced in \cite{zhang_diffaero_2025}, which has inspired the simulator design used in this work.
The simulator supports different training tasks such as hovering, position control, and obstacle avoidance; all tasks are differentiable and the simulator supports parallel environment execution for faster data collection.
Similarly, \cite{heeg_learning_2025} presents an efficient differentiable quadrotor framework implemented in JAX~\cite{jax2018github} for learning hover stabilization from visual features, and compares \ac{BPTT} optimization against standard \ac{PPO}.
Building on this line of work, \cite{pan_learning_2026, ren_learning_2026} demonstrate real-time policy adaptation using real-world data, leveraging the improved sample efficiency of differentiable methods.

On the other hand, another research direction relevant for this thesis, aims to answer the question of how to tune the gains of a given controller to achieve optimal performance.
The authors of \cite{loquercio_autotune_2022} show that the faster a trajectory to be tracked is, the more sensitive is the tracking performance to the controller gains.
This way, they propose an algorithm based on the Metropolis-Hastings sampling \cite{metropolis_equation_1953}, to tune the gains of a \ac{MPC} controller for different segements of a trajectory.
The trajectory is divded in segments with similar height gradients and minimum time of 2 s per segment. 
Their approach showed an increase on trajectory tracking succes comparted to another automatic gain tuning method based on genetic algorithms~\cite{khuwaja_pid_2018}.

\section{Objectives and Structure}

The primary objective of this thesis is to create a differentiable quadrotor simulator.
We aim to backpropagate gradients from a given cost function to the \ac{NN} parameters to autonomously command a drone, in the context of \ac{ADR}.
Additionally, we propose a control architecture where a low-level geometric controller accepts velocity and heading-rate commands from the \ac{NN} and maps them to wrench (collective force and torques) setpoints.
Moreover, we augment the policy so that it can tune its inner $\mathrm{SO}(3)$ geometric controller by outputting the controller gains, and we compare this to the fixed-gains baseline.

As secondary objectives, we perform an evaluation of the policy network in Gazebo utilizing the MRS UAV ecosystem~\cite{baca_mrs_2021}, and we test the controller on a real platform.
Furthermore, we perform a comparative study between different control modalities, and between model-free and model-based optimization methods.

The thesis is divided into six chapters.
In \autoref{chap:introduction} we introduce the thesis topic and review the most relevant related works.
In \autoref{pre} we cover the theoretical material on which this thesis is built.
In \autoref{meth} we present the methodology: the learning environment, the quadrotor model, the implemented control modalities, the domain randomization strategy, and the cost functions to be minimized for two racing tasks.
In \autoref{chap:results} we show and analyze the results of our method and perform comparative studies.
We analyze the effect of different control abstractions on sample efficiency and trajectory-tracking performance.
Then, we compare \ac{BPTT} against \ac{PPO} on the gate-progress task.
We study the effect of our implementation decisions on transferability to Gazebo and to a real-world platform.
In \autoref{chap:conclusion} we discuss the results and provide a conclusion.
Finally, in \autoref{chap:fut_dir} we state our future research directions.
