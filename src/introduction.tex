\ac{ADR} has emerged as a compelling platform in robotics for developing and deploying novel control techniques in real-world scenarios.
The aim of this sport is to navigate through a sequence of gates in minimum time, pushing the platform’s capabilities to their limits~\cite{hanover_autonomous_2024}.
To maintain agility and low weight, the hardware is kept minimal; a typical \ac{ADR} drone consists of a quadrotor equipped with an RGB camera, an \ac{IMU}, an onboard computer, a flight controller, brushless \ac{DC} motors with propellers, and \ac{ESC} all powered by a \ac{LiPo} battery~\cite{foehn_agilicious_2022}.

\ac{ADR} presents several challenges, which can be divided into the following subgroups depicted in \autoref{fig:intro}.

    \centering

    \label{fig:intro}
\end{figure}

The control stack begins at the \textbf{perception} level.
This stage involves processing sensory data from the camera and IMU, which is inherently noisy and uncertain.
The main challenge at this level is to filter and fuse this data to estimate both the position of the gates and the state of the drone.
The fusion of camera and IMU measurements is known as \ac{VIO}.
State estimation is typically performed using filtering techniques that combine a system model with sensor measurements, such as the \ac{EKF} or variants.
Given an estimate of the drone state and the environment, the next step is \textbf{planning}.
The objective in \ac{ADR} is to compute a minimum-time trajectory that passes through the gates while avoiding collisions.
Trajectory planning can be computationally demanding, making efficient approaches particularly important.
The final stage of the pipeline is \textbf{control}.
Given a planned trajectory, the goal is to determine the control inputs that track it with minimal error.
This task is challenging due to the highly nonlinear dynamics of the quadrotor.
Additionally, the system is underactuated, with only four out of the six \ac{DOFs} directly controllable.
Moreover, yaw control authority is typically limited, and actuator saturation further constrains performance.

Many of these challenges are common in robotics, and a variety of methods have been proposed to address them.
Learning-based methods, particularly those relying on neural networks, are gaining popularity due to their low-latency inference and robustness to noise.
Individual components of the navigation stack can be learned using neural networks.
For instance, \ac{CNNs} can process \ac{VIO} data to detect gates, while \ac{MLPs}  can be used for planning or control.
Combinations of multiple stages, such as perception and planning or planning and control, can also be learned jointly.
In some cases, the entire pipeline is learned end-to-end.
Learning-based approaches offer several advantages; however, training these models typically requires large amounts of data, and current optimization methods can be sample inefficient.
Since collecting such data in real-world settings is often impractical, simulated environments that approximate real-world distributions are commonly used, enabling thousands of parallel training instances.
Recent research has also explored the use of \ac{ADE} in \ac{ML} frameworks to propagate gradients from a cost function to policy parameters.
This approach significantly improves sample efficiency and avoids treating the quadrotor dynamics as a black box.

In this thesis, we investigate this direction by focusing on the use of differentiability to learn the control component of the navigation stack.
We aim to demonstrate that this approach is a promising avenue for research in robotics and particulalry in \ac{ADR}.

\section{Related Work}

The evolution of \ac{ADR} has progressed from early academic benchmarks in the IROS competitions between 2016 and 2018 \cite{iros2016, iros2017, iros2018}, where drones reached speeds of only a few meters per second, to highly professionalized racing events since 2019, where autonomous drones can exceed 100~km/h and outperform professional FPV pilots.
A major breakthrough occurred during the \textit{AlphaPilot} competition in 2019, where teams achieved speeds close to 10~m/s, and more recently in the A2RL Drone Champions League \cite{a2rl}, where autonomous systems surpassed human champions.
These milestones were enabled by advances in perception, planning, control, and learning-based methods.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \resizebox{1.0\textwidth}{!}{\input{fig/tikz/timeline.tex}}
    \caption{The modular navigation stack for ADR and its associated challenges.}
    \label{fig:introo}
\end{figure}

Since the AlphaPilot competition \cite{lockheedmartinAlphaPilotDrone}, there has been a clear trend toward incorporating learning-based components into the navigation stack.
In \cite{foehnalphapilot2021}, the pipeline combined \ac{VIO} and gate detections within an \ac{EKF} for state estimation, followed by trajectory planning using sampling-based receding horizon methods.
Control was implemented through a cascade of position and attitude controllers, while neural networks were used exclusively for visual gate detection.
Subsequent work in \cite{kaufmannchampion-level2023} retained a similar perception pipeline but replaced planning and control with a feedforward \ac{MLP}, optimized using \ac{PPO} \cite{ppo}.
In a related setup, \cite{songreaching2023} demonstrated that model-free \ac{RL} could outperform traditional optimal control approaches.

More recent approaches have explored model-based \ac{RL}, where a learned world model enables training through imagined rollouts.
In \cite{romero_dream_2025, verraest_skydreamer_2025}, the authors adopt an actor--critic architecture inspired by \cite{hafner_mastering_2024}.
While these methods achieve strong performance, they suffer from high training times and limited sample efficiency; for instance, training in \cite{verraest_skydreamer_2025} can take up to 50 hours, significantly slowing down the research cycle.

To address these limitations, recent work has shifted toward differentiable approaches that enable backpropagation of gradients from an objective function through the entire navigation stack, this is known as \ac{BPTT}.
This paradigm avoids treating the quadrotor dynamics as a black box and substantially improves sample efficiency.
The work of \cite{zhang_back_2025} pioneered this direction, achieving agile vision-based flight in cluttered environments using a \ac{CRNN} and a simplified double-integrator model.
A differentiable simulation framework is introduced in \cite{zhang_diffaero_2025}, which has inspired the simulator design used in this work.
Similarly, \cite{heeg_learning_2025} presents an efficient differentiable quadrotor framework implemented in JAX for learning stabilization from visual features, and compares it against standard \ac{PPO}.
Building on this line of work, \cite{pan_learning_2026, ren_learning_2026} demonstrate real-time policy adaptation using real-world data, leveraging the improved sample efficiency of differentiable methods.
