\section{Simulator Validation}\label{sim-val}
As detailed in \autoref{sec:quad_model}, our simulator utilizes a simplified model of quadrotor dynamics and aerodynamics. 
To maintain a computationally efficient and generalizable framework, we do not model battery depletion on thrust, complex aerodynamic effects, and the propellers rotational inertia. 
While these unmodeled dynamics can cumulatively lead to a significant sim-to-real gap, we aim to address this mismatch through \ac{DR}.
To validate the reliability of this simplified approach, we benchmark our simulator's behavior against Gazebo, a high-fidelity physics engine widely adopted by the robotics community. 
In particular, we utilize the MRS UAV ecosystem~\cite{baca_mrs_2021}.

We separately assess the responses of the body rates to a unit step, a variable-amplitude signal, and a variable-frequency signal.
\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=1.0\textwidth]{./fig/plots/omega-xy-val/hypotheses.pdf}
  \caption{Pitch/Roll responses to: \textbf{Left}: unit-step signal. \textbf{Middle}: variable-amplitude signal. \textbf{Right}: variable-frequency signal.}
  \label{fig:wx-y-val}
\end{figure}
For the pitch and roll responses, the behavior observed in Gazebo and in our simulator is consistent.
Minor discrepancies can be observed in the response of integration of the unit-step error, together with a slight overshoot in the variable-frequency signal.
\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=1.0\textwidth]{./fig/plots/omega-z-val/hypotheses.pdf}
  \caption{Yaw response to: \textbf{Left}: unit-step signal. \textbf{Middle}: variable-amplitude signal. \textbf{Right}: variable-frequency signal.}
  \label{fig:wz-val}
\end{figure}

Similarly, the yaw responses between simulators are close, but some mismatch in the unit-step and variable-frequency signals can be observed.
In our simulator, we saturate the yaw rate to $\pm 3$ rad/s and the yaw acceleration to $\pm 5$ rad/$s^2$ to match Gazebo's behavior.

\section{Evaluation Criteria}\label{sec:eval_criteria}

This section details the metrics used to evaluate our results. 
The evaluation is structured around three primary axes: training/sample efficiency, performance; in terms of lap time and \ac{SR}, and \ac{S2S} transferability. 

\subsection{Sample Efficiency}
One of the central claims of this thesis is that leveraging analytical gradients from differentiable simulation significantly improves sample efficiency compared to standard model-free \ac{RL} algorithms.
To evaluate this claim, the training convergence of the proposed differentiable physics approach is compared against \ac{PPO} trained on the same task, observation space, network architecture, and control abstraction.
Sample efficiency is measured as the convergence of the obtained mean reward against the simulated steps. 
We consider an optimization method more sample efficent than other if the mean reward converges for a lower number of simulation steps.
Convergence is not strictly defined here; it is assessed qualitatively by inspecting the learning curves and noting when the mean reward stops increasing significantly.

\subsection{Performance}
\subsubsection{Lap Time and Success Rate}

Ultimately, the primary objective of \ac{ADR} is to traverse a sequence of gates as fast as possible.
The metrics used to evaluate this objective are the lap time $T_f$, the gate \ac{SR}, and the track \ac{SR}.
The lap time $T_f$ is defined as the time elapsed from crossing the first gate to crossing it again.
The gate \ac{SR} is defined as the ratio of successfully passed gates $G_p$ to the total number of crossed gates $G$,

\begin{equation}
    \mathit{SR}_{\text{gate}} = 100\cdot\frac{G_p}{G} \in [0, 100]\ \% 
\end{equation}
while the track \ac{SR} indicates how many of the laps performed did not have a single gate crash,

\begin{equation}
    \mathit{SR}_{\text{track}} = 100\cdot\frac{L_c}{L} \in [0, 100]\ \%
\end{equation}
where $L_c$ is the number of completed laps without a crash and $L$ is the total number of laps performed.


\subsubsection{Root Mean Square Error}
When a reference trajectory is used, the performance metric is the \ac{RMSE} between the drone's state and the reference state.
We consider only the position \ac{RMSE},
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{RMSE} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{N} \sum_{k=1}^N \left\|\mathbf{p}_k - \mathbf{p}^{\mathrm{ref}}_k\right\|_2^2 },
\end{equation}
and velocity \ac{RMSE},
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{RMSE} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{N} \sum_{k=1}^N \left\|\mathbf{v}_k - \mathbf{v}^{\mathrm{ref}}_k\right\|_2^2 }.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Transferability and Robustness}\label{subsec:transfer}

% Learned policies are prone to overfitting to the training environment and may fail to generalize when deployed under conditions that deviate from those seen during training, due to model mismatches and unmodeled effects.
To assess transferability, the policies trained in the proposed differentiable simulator are evaluated in a high-fidelity simulation environment using the MRS UAV stack in Gazebo~\cite{baca_mrs_2021}.
Since the differentiable physics and model-free \ac{RL} policies are optimized under different paradigms, the \ac{S2S} performance gap is analyzed for each method, measuring the degradation in \ac{SR} and lap time $T_f$ relative to the results obtained in the training simulator.
Additionally, robustness to external disturbances is evaluated by exposing the trained policies to wind perturbations during deployment, without any retraining or fine-tuning.
The evaluation of robustness is carried out in our simulation environment.

\section{PX4 Mixing Logic}\label{px4-mixing}
In this appendix section, we explain in further detail how the PX4 firmware maps collective thrust and body-rate setpoints to motor commands~\cite{px4_ctrl}, and how the mixer handles desaturation~\cite{px4_desat}.
Unlike how we initially modeled it in \autoref{ctbr-bad}, the PX4 rate controller consists of a \ac{PID}, where the torque is obtained as,

\begin{equation}\label{px4-tau}
  \boldsymbol{\tau}_{\text{cmd}} = \mathbf{k_p} (\boldsymbol{\omega}_{\text{cmd}} - \boldsymbol{\omega}) + \mathbf{rate\_int} + \mathbf{k_d} \boldsymbol{\alpha} + \mathbf{k_{ff}} \boldsymbol{\omega}_{\text{cmd}}
\end{equation}
where $\boldsymbol{\omega}_\text{cmd}$ is computed as in \eqref{omega-cmd} and  $\boldsymbol{\alpha}$ is the dron's angular accelaration obtained from the \ac{IMU}.
where $\boldsymbol{\omega}_\text{cmd}$ is computed as in \eqref{omega-cmd} and $\boldsymbol{\alpha}$ is the drone's angular acceleration obtained from the \ac{IMU}.
Since we do not model any sensors, we approximate the angular acceleration as $ \alpha = (\boldsymbol{\omega}_k - \boldsymbol{\omega}_{k-1}) / \Delta t$.
The gains of the rate controller are:

\begin{table}[H]
  \centering
  \caption{PX4 rate controller gains.}
  \begin{tabular}{l|c}
    \hline
    Gain & Value \\ \hline
    $\mathbf{k_p}$ & $(0.042,\ 0.042,\ 0.2)$ \\
    $\mathbf{k_i}$ & $(0.08,\ 0.08,\ 0.1)$ \\
    $\mathbf{k_d}$ & $(0.0015,\ 0.0015,\ 0.0)$ \\
    $\mathbf{k_{ff}}$ & $(0.0,\ 0.0,\ 0.0)$ \\
    $\mathbf{lim\_int}$ & $(0.30,\ 0.30,\ 0.30)$ \\
    \hline
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}
\noindent The integral term of \autoref{px4-tau}, is computed as,

\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{rate\_int} += \text{max}(1 - (\frac{\boldsymbol{\omega}_{\text{cmd}} - \boldsymbol{\omega}}{400})^2,\ 0) \mathbf{k_i} (\boldsymbol{\omega}_{\text{cmd}} - \boldsymbol{\omega})\Delta t.
\end{equation}
Furthermore, it is clamped element-wise to remain within the configured integral limits,

\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{rate\_int} = \text{clip}(\mathbf{rate\_int},\ \mathbf{-lim\_int},\ \mathbf{lim\_int}).
\end{equation}
And the collective throttle is directly given by the policy as,
\begin{equation}
  throttle = u_0. 
\end{equation}
When mapping from wrench (collective force and torques) to motor commands using \eqref{eq:simp_alloc}, the solution can include saturated actuator setpoints or even negative thrust commands. Since the motors are not reversible, the solution has to be desaturated.
The PX4 firmware allows three desaturation modes depending on the value of the parameter MC\_AIRMODE. By default, it is set to 0, which means airmode is disabled; if set to 1, airmode is enabled for pitch and roll; and if set to 2, airmode is enabled for pitch, roll, and yaw.

As illustrated in \autoref{airmode}, if airmode is enabled, increasing the thrust is allowed in order to keep the commanded torques.
\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=1.0\textwidth]{fig/photos/airmode.png}
  \caption{ Saturation mixing when airmode is disabled or enabled (from \cite{px4}). }
  \label{airmode}
\end{figure}

% with the commanded throttle and torques, the mixing to obtain the motor commands proceeds as follows.
Desaturation is done in a normalized space, $motor_i \in [0, 1]\ i = 1, \dots, 4$, so only the signs of the allocation matrix, \eqref{eq:simp_alloc}, are used to obtain the motor setpoints, which for our quadrotor configuration (see \autoref{f-diag}) correspond to:

\begin{equation}
  \begin{aligned}
    motor\_1 &= u_0 - \tau_{b_1} - \tau_{b_2} - \tau_{b_3} \\
    motor\_2 &= u_0 - \tau_{b_1} + \tau_{b_2} + \tau_{b_3} \\
    motor\_3 &= u_0 + \tau_{b_1} + \tau_{b_2} - \tau_{b_3} \\
    motor\_4 &= u_0 + \tau_{b_1} - \tau_{b_2} + \tau_{b_3}
    \label{mixing}
  \end{aligned}
\end{equation}

%   \begin{aligned}
%     motor\_1 &= u_0 - \tau_{b_1} - \tau_{b_2} \\
%     motor\_2 &= u_0 - \tau_{b_1} + \tau_{b_2} \\
%     motor\_3 &= u_0 + \tau_{b_1} + \tau_{b_2} \\
%     motor\_4 &= u_0 + \tau_{b_1} - \tau_{b_2}
%   \end{aligned}

Note that if MC\_AIRMODE is 0 or 1, the yaw torques are not yet included in the mixing. 
The mixing of pitch and roll is prioritized and then the yaw is mixed.
The desaturation cascade starts with the collective thrust.
And the desaturation algorithm consists of two steps.
The algorithm takes the motor setpoints and the desaturation vector, which is the sign of the corresponding row of the allocation matrix (i.e., first row for collective thrust, second for pitch, ...). Then, the desaturation gain is computed as follows. 
For each actuator $i$ with desaturation vector component $d_i \neq 0$, a candidate gain $k_i$ is computed only if that actuator's setpoint $m_i$ violates its bounds $[motor_i^{min}, motor_i^{max}]$

\begin{equation}
  k_i =
  \begin{cases}
    \dfrac{motor_i^{min} - motor_i}{d_i}, & \text{if } motor_i < motor_i^{min} \\[8pt]
    \dfrac{motor_i^{max} - motor_i}{d_i}, & \text{if } motor_i > motor_i^{max}
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:desat_candidate}
\end{equation}

Actuators within the bounds are not considered, since no correction is needed.
Each $k_i$ is exactly the scalar gain that, if applied, would place actuator $i$ inside its operating boundary. 
The most extreme candidates in each direction are then retained,

\begin{equation}
  k_{min} = \min\left(0,\ \min_i k_i\right), \qquad
  k_{max} = \max\left(0,\ \max_i k_i\right)
  \label{eq:desat_extremes}
\end{equation}
and the final desaturation gain is their sum,

\begin{equation}
  k = k_{min} + k_{max}.
  \label{eq:desat_gain}
\end{equation}

Once the gain $k$ from \autoref{eq:desat_gain} is computed, if $k_1 > 0$ and only decreases are permitted along $\mathbf{d}$, $k_1$ is discarded (set to zero) and no correction is applied for this vector $\mathbf{d}$.

% it is applied to the motor setpoints in two successive steps rather than a single correction.
% In the first step, the full gain $k_1 = k$ is applied,

\begin{equation}
  motor_i^\prime = motor_i + k_1 d_i, \qquad \forall i
  \label{eq:desat_step1}
\end{equation}

Otherwise, the motor setpoints are corrected, \eqref{eq:desat_step1}. 
Since $k$ is a single scalar gain, conflicts in saturation can still exist; applying $k_1$ once does not, in general, bring every actuator inside the bounds, and some actuators may remain saturated, or previously unsaturated actuators may now become saturated after the correction. 
For this reason, the desaturation gain is recomputed on the updated setpoints $motor_i^\prime$, and a second, half-magnitude correction is applied,

\begin{equation}
  k_2 = \dfrac{1}{2}\,k(\text{motor}_i^{\prime}), \qquad
  motor_i^{\prime\prime} = motor_i^\prime + k_2 d_i, \qquad \forall i
  \label{eq:desat_step2}
\end{equation}

% where $\text{motor}_i^\prime$ denotes the setpoints after \autoref{eq:desat_step1}. 
The factor $\tfrac{1}{2}$ damps the correction to avoid overshooting the boundary that the first step may have caused.

In our simulation and during deployment in Gazebo, we selected MC\_AIRMODE=0, that is, airmode disabled, so first, the collective thrust is desaturated with only gains $k<0$.
Then roll is desaturated followed by pitch, this time, allowing positive desaturation gains.
Finally, yaw is introduced in \eqref{mixing}, and yaw is desaturated, followed by a last desaturation of the collective thrust, in case it becomes saturated again after introducing yaw into the mixing.
This procedure mimics the original implementation~\cite{px4_desat}.

% When  restriction discarded $k_1$ in the first step (i.e. $k_1 = 0$ because only an increase was available and increases are not allowed), the second step is skipped entirely as well: no partial correction is applied along $\mathbf{d}$, and the saturation on that axis is left for the subsequent stages of the cascade (roll, pitch, and finally yaw) to resolve.
