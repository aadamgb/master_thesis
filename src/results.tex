\chapter{Results\label{chap:results}}

To investigate how \ac{DR} affects the transferability of controllers learned in simulation to different environments, the following experiment was designed.
First, three randomized drone instances were initialized in simulation, and a common policy was executed concurrently in Gazebo during 5 minutes to< measure the \acp{UAV} lap times and success rates.
The policies were trained to race in a simple eight-figure track (see \autoref{t:track}) and their dynamical parameters can be found in \autoref{uav_types}.
This procedure was performed for four distinct policies trained at randomization levels of 0\%, 10\%, 20\%, and 30\%.
The results of this first experiment are presented in \autoref{simres}.

\section{Training to Simulation Results}\label{simres}

As depicted in \autoref{sim:0DR}, the baseline configuration with 0\% randimization yields to the fastest avarage lap time of 4.13 s.
However, this speed comes with a significat cost in realaiability.
More than half of the laps resulted in a gate crash and two of the drones fatally crashed druing the experiment.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    % \includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth, height=0.25\linewidth]{fig/photos/sim_results_10DR.png} 
    \includegraphics[scale=0.35]{fig/photos/lap_times_0DR.eps} 
    \caption{0\% randomized policy simulated results.} 
    \label{sim:0DR}
\end{figure}

Randomizing the dynamical parameters at 10\% produced a notable increase in average lap times to 5.79 s, \autoref{sim:10DR}.
Nonetheless, reliability and robustness improved significantly, with all three drones completing the experiment without fatal crashes and success rates exceeding 97\%.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    % \includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth, height=0.25\linewidth]{fig/photos/sim_results_10DR.png} 
    \includegraphics[scale=0.35]{fig/photos/lap_times_10DR.eps}
    \caption{10\% randomized policy simulated results.}  
    \label{sim:10DR}
\end{figure}

A further increase in randomization to 20\% resulted in slightly reduced lap times. 
It is worth noting that training is a stochastic process; training two policies with identical initialization parameters yield to different results. 
Among all trained policies, the 20\% randomized policy achieved the best overall performance with an average lap time of 5.38 s and a perfect success rate across all three drones, as shown in \autoref{sim:20DR}.
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    % \includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth, height=0.25\linewidth]{fig/photos/sim_results_10DR.png} 
    \includegraphics[scale=0.35]{fig/photos/lap_times_20DR.eps}
    \caption{20\% randomized policy simulated results.}  
    \label{sim:20DR}
\end{figure}

Finally, training with 30\% randomization achieved acceptable results for UAV1 and UAV2, as shown in \autoref{sim:30DR}.
However, for UAV3, the policy failed to reliably control the drone.
As discussed in \autoref{how_to}, we hypothesize that excessive randomization can destabilize training, worsening sample efficiency and requiring more convergence time.


\begin{figure}[H]

The selected randomization methodology randomizes all dynamical parameters uniformly, but some critical parameters such as the thrust map may require more restrictive bounds to prevent physically infeasible configurations.
