\chapter{Results}\label{chap:results}

In this chapter, the results of our methodology are presented and evaluated.
The definitions of the assessment metrics used throughout the chapter can be found in \refapp{sec:eval_criteria}.
In \autoref{sec:sur_grad} we start by analysing the effect of using a surrogate gradient on the quality of the optimization signal.
Next, in \autoref{sec:ca_eval} we evaluate how the control abstractions that we have implemented affect the task of trajectory tracking, and in \autoref{sec:realworld} we show data from an experiment carried out on a real \ac{UAV} platform.
Subsequently, in \autoref{sec:mb-vs-mf} we evaluate whether our implementation of \ac{BPTT} is better than standard \ac{PPO} for the gate progress task.
Finally, in \autoref{adapt_gains} we assess if allowing the policy to output the gains of a geometric controller leads to improvements in a racing scenario.
The performance of the proposed method is compared against standard model-free \ac{RL} training and a classical fixed-gain geometric controller.




\section{Surrogate Gradient Analysis}\label{sec:sur_grad}
In this section, we analyze the effect of using surrogate gradients during the policy optimization process.
The gradient is backpropagated from the loss function to the policy parameters through the chain rule.
As discussed in \autoref{sec:diff-opt}, the gradients are computed numerically from a computational graph.
Certain operations, such as \eqref{eq:motspd}, can be highly sensitive to small perturbations, leading to unstable gradients and making the optimization process more difficult.
This behaviour can be observed in \autoref{fig:gradients}, where the loss and gradient norm are plotted against the training steps for two different approaches: a simplified backpropagated model that omits motor dynamics, and the full model including the motor dynamics.
At the beginning of training, both approaches exhibit similar behavior.
However, the full model eventually converges to a suboptimal solution and fails to track the trajectory successfully.
By analyzing the gradient norm of the full model, larger oscillations can be observed, which we believe are the cause for the unstable optimization and suboptimal convergence.

\section{Control Abstraction Evaluation}\label{sec:ca_eval}
In this section, we evaluate how the implemented control abstractions affect the sample efficiency, trajectory tracking performance, as well as the transferability and robustness of the control policies (see \refapp{sec:eval_criteria}).

\subsection{Sample Efficiency}\label{subsec:sample_eff}
To evaluate sample efficiency of the control abstractions, the four policies are trained for 40 million steps to track random harmonic trajectories (see, \eqref{harmonics}).
The training conditions are identical for all policies; including the same randomization settings, loss function, network architecture, learning rate, and other hyperparameters.
We then analyze how the loss evolves throughout training.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics{fig/plots/sample-eff-ca/hypotheses.pdf}
    \caption{Loss over time steps for the different control abstractions.}
    \label{samp-eff-ca}
\end{figure}

From \autoref{samp-eff-ca} it can be observed that the \ac{SRT} policy requires the longest training time to converge, since the network must implicitly learn the mapping between motor commands and body rates.
Although this approach is slower to train, it has the advantage of requiring no controller gains or manual tuning.
In contrast, the \ac{CTBR} policy converges significantly faster, reaching convergence after approximately 1.5 million steps.
The geometric control abstractions, \ac{LVHR} and \ac{LVHR}+g, converge even faster, requiring only around 0.5 million steps.
Despite their improved convergence speed, it's worth noting that the latter policies rely on carefully tuned gains to ensure stable behavior, which can be time-consuming and task-dependent.

\subsection{Performance}\label{sec:perff}
To evaluate performance, the policies are further trained on a specific racing track.
The selected track is commonly used in the literature, as it contains straight sections, high-curvature segments, and a split-S gate configuration, making it suitable for benchmarking agile flight performance.
This circuit is commonly referred to as the UZH track, as it was designed by the University of Zurich.
To generate trajectories passing through the gates, the time-optimal gate traversal planner\footnote{The trajectory planner code can be found in: \url{https://github.com/FSC-Lab/TOGT-Planner}} proposed in~\cite{qin_time-optimal_2024} is used.
The planner generates trajectories that account for both the gate geometry and the quadrotor dynamics.

Training directly on the optimal trajectory consistently resulted in failure to track the reference.
We hypothesize that the optimization becomes trapped in local minima, preventing the policy from converging to a stable solution.
To facilitate learning, a curriculum-based training strategy is adopted.
Initially, the trajectory speed is scaled down to 10\% of the original reference speed.
Once an episode achieves an RMSE below $0.15$~m, the trajectory speed is increased by an additional 10\%.
Using this approach, the policies are able to track the trajectory at up to 60\% of the original speed, which remains significantly below the optimal performance.
This limitation requires further investigation, particularly regarding the design of the loss function and the neural network architecture.
In \autoref{tab:uzh_ca} and \autoref{fig:uzh_ca} we present the tracking results of the four control abstractions on the UZH track at 60\% of the reference speed, where the policies achieve a maximum speed of 10 m/s.

\section{Model-Based and Model-Free Evaluation}\label{sec:mb-vs-mf}
In this section, we compare two policies, one optimized using model-free \ac{RL} with \ac{PPO}, and the other using model-based \ac{RL} with \ac{BPTT}.
The comparison is performed in terms of sample efficiency, performance, robustness and transferability (see \refapp{sec:eval_criteria}).



We analyze two scenarios.
First, we train the policies to pass through one gate located at $\mathbf{p_g}^\mathcal{W} = \begin{pmatrix}
    0.0 & 5.0 & 1.5
\end{pmatrix}$. The \ac{UAV}s' state is initialized to zero, unless for $z = 1.5$ and $q_\omega = 1.0$ which correspond to the height of the gate and identity orientation respectively.
Second, we train on a more challenging track, the Eight figure track.
We maintain the same reward for both optimization methods, which consists of rewarding progress toward the gate and penalizing body rates as,

\begin{equation} \label{reward_fnc}
   r_k = -\mathcal{L}_k = \left\| \mathbf{p}_{k-1}^\mathcal{W} - \mathbf{p}_{g_k}^\mathcal{W} \right\|_2 - \left\| \mathbf{p}_{k}^\mathcal{W} - \mathbf{p}_{g_k}^\mathcal{W} \right\|_2 - 0.001 \left\| \boldsymbol{\omega} \right\|_2.
\end{equation}

We run the simulation with 100 parallel environments.
Note that with \ac{BPTT} we aim to minimize the loss and in \ac{PPO} to maximize the reward, but these are equivalent objectives.

\subsection{Sample Efficiency}

To analyze the sample efficiency, we plot the mean reward over the parallel environments against the collected simulation steps for both methods and scenarios.


% here
In \autoref{fig:eprew}, we can observe that for the simple one-gate setup, both policies converge to a reward of around $4.0$.
However, the \ac{BPTT} policy converges in around $0.5\times 10^{6}$ steps while the \ac{PPO} policy does so around $2.5\times 10^{6}$ steps,
making the \ac{BPTT} method $\mathbf{5x}$ more sample efficient in this simple scenario.
Nonetheless, analyzing the more challenging Eight figure track, the \ac{PPO} policy significantly outperforms the \ac{BPTT} policy.
The \ac{PPO} policy achieves a maximum reward of $63.4$ with smooth policy updates, while \ac{BPTT} does not surpass $50.2$ and presents high variance in the reward.
Although both are optimized with a learning rate of $\alpha = 3\times 10^{-4}$.
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics{fig/plots/sample-eff-opt2/hypotheses.pdf}
    \caption{Reward against time step for PPO and BPTT. \textbf{Left:} One gate scenario. \textbf{Rigth:} Eight figure track. }
    \label{fig:eprew}
\end{figure}
We believe that this difference in the maximum achieved reward is due to the relative simplicity of our \ac{BPTT} implementation compared to the actor-critic architecture and robust optimization scheme of \ac{PPO}.

The \ac{BPTT} policy is optimized by backpropagating gradients directly through the differentiable simulator, using a single feedforward network with no value function.
While this direct approach shows higher sample efficiency for a simple scenario, when introducing multiple gates the optimization space is less smooth.
This increases the risk of converging prematurely to a local optimum.
Additionally, the \ac{BPTT} policy appears to be optimized greedily, since the optimization is performed over a horizon of only 100 steps and therefore does not fully account for long-horizon reward.
We select the optimization horizon $H=100$ and learning rate $\alpha=3\times10^{-4}$ based on the experimental result depicted in \autoref{heatmap}, since this combination provides the highest mean episode reward.
% \todo{Heatmap plot with x:lr and y: H ??, to show best combination for eight track?}
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics{fig/plots/heatmap/hypotheses.pdf}
    \caption{Best mean episode reward over 500 espisodes and 800 steps for combinations of optimization horizon $H = [25,\ 50,\, 100,\ 200,\ 400,\ 800]$ and learning rates $\alpha = [3\times10^{-5},\, 3\times10^{-4},\ 3\times10^{-3},\ 3\times10^{-2}]$.}
    \label{heatmap}

In contrast, \ac{PPO} levereages a critic network that maps the value of a given state, reduceing the variance of the advantage estimates used to update the actor.
Furthermore, the clipped surrogate objective constrains each policy update to remain within a trust region.
Combined with multiple epochs of minibatch stochastic gradient updates per rollout, this yields more conservative and robust policy updates than the single-step gradient descent used in our \ac{BPTT} implementation.

\section{Fixed Gains Versus Adaptive Gains} \label{adapt_gains}

Lastly, we investigate in further detail whether having adaptive gains for the geometric controller leads to an increase in performance.
The policies were optimized with \ac{PPO} since it showed superior performance to our \ac{BPTT} approach, and we trained on the same Eight figure track as before.
Both controllers can output linear velocities in a range of $\mathbf{v} \in [-\mathbf{v}_{\text{max}}, \mathbf{v}_{\text{max}}]$, where $\mathbf{v}_{\text{max}} = (10, 10, 3)\,\mathrm{m/s}$.
The fixed gain policy has a proportional gain in velocity of $\mathbf{k}_v = (3, 3, 4)$, and a gain in attitude of $\mathbf{k}_R = (8, 8, 1)$.
The adaptive policy selects these gains dynamically at each time step in the ranges of:
\begin{align*}
    \mathbf{k}_v^{\min} &= (0.5, 0.5, 0.5), & \mathbf{k}_v^{\max} &= (6.0, 6.0, 8.0), \\
    \mathbf{k}_R^{\min} &= (1, 1, 0.2), & \mathbf{k}_R^{\max} &= (16, 16, 2).
\end{align*}


Note that, contrary to \autoref{subsec:lvhr}, the gains are different per axis (pitch, roll, yaw) and, instead of commanding body torques, the controller commands body rates such that \eqref{eq:geo_tau} becomes \eqref{geo-omega}.
The commanded collective thrust and attitude $\mathbf{R}_{\text{cmd}}$ are computed in the same manner from the velocity commands.


\begin{equation}\label{geo-omega}
% \mathbf{\omega}_{\text{des}}^{\text{world}} = \begin{bmatrix}0\\0\\\dot{\psi}\end{bmatrix},\quad
% \mathbf{\omega}_{\text{ff}} = R^{\top} R_{\text{des}}\,\mathbf{\omega}_{\text{des}}^{\text{world}},\quad
\boldsymbol{\omega}_{\text{cmd}} = -\mathbf{k}_R \mathbf{e}_R + \boldsymbol{\omega}_{\text{ff}}
\end{equation}
Where, 

\begin{equation}
     \boldsymbol{\omega}_{\text{ff}} = \mathbf{R}^\top \mathbf{R}_{\text{cmd}}\boldsymbol{\dot{\eta}}_{\text{cmd}}.
\end{equation}
This modification improved stability during training and deployment.
\subsection{Sample Efficiency}
Investigating the sample efficiency of the fixed-gain and adaptive-gain geometric controllers, we can see in \autoref{fig:samp-eff-geo} that the former converges at around 30 million steps, while the latter converges at around 70 million.
The fixed-gain controller achieves a maximum reward of 53.29, while the adaptive controller achieves a maximum reward of 60.12.

Moreover, around step 80 in \autoref{fig:lvhr-g-action-ppo}, the policy goes from $10\ \mathrm{m/s}$ to $-10\ \mathrm{m/s}$ in the $x$ direction. 
A few time steps before this change, the policy starts decreasing the gain $k_{v_x}$, and once $-10\ \mathrm{m/s}$ is reached, the gain is increased again.
A similar behavior can be observed with the $\mathbf{k}_R$ gains, where the pitch and roll attitude gains appear to be more synchronized.
This predicting behaviour can explain the superior perfomance of the adaptive policy in our simulation environment.

\subsection{Robustness}
To evaluate the robustness of the controllers, we perform the same experiment as in \autoref{robust}.
The experiment shows that the fixed-gain policy can withstand wind disturbances of up to $7\ \mathrm{m/s^2}$ with a perfect success rate, while the adaptive-gain policy can handle up to $8\ \mathrm{m/s^2}$, indicating that both policies achieve approximately the same robustness.


\subsection{Transferability}
Contrary to \autoref{tab:geo-adapt-fixed-perf}, where the adaptive policy outperformed the fixed policy, deployment in Gazebo shows opposite results.
\autoref{tab:geo-adapt-fixed} and \autoref{fig:lap_times_geo} show that the fixed-gain policy transfers better than the adaptive policy.
The fixed policy has faster lap times, and $83.33\%$ of its laps are successful, whereas the adaptive policy crashed into the ground at lap 18, with only 9 laps completed successfully.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{fig/photos/so3-god.png}
        \caption{Fixed gains geometric policy deployed in Gazebo (Eight figure track).}
    \label{fig:gazebo}
\end{figure}


We believe that this performance mismatch between our simulator and Gazebo is due to several unmodeled factors that we could not faithfully capture.
In our simulator, we attempt to replicate the PX4 firmware and to mimic how the flight computer handles the commands, we integrate the dynamics at $1000\ \mathrm{Hz}$, while the inner rate control loop in the PX4 software runs at $250\ \mathrm{Hz}$ and the policy at $100\ \mathrm{Hz}$.
Following this approach, the policies transferred better; however, model mismatches could still be present.
Additionally, the adaptive geometric policy is more aggressive yet more sensitive to the commanded gains, whose inappropriate combinations can lead to instabilities that cause the drone to crash.
